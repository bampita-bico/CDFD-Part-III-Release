# Mujjabi Biology Laws and Tests

This file names the Part III biological translations used across the manuscript
spine. The names are release vocabulary; their empirical status remains open
until independently validated.

## Biology Capacity Law

When sustained flow `Phi` approaches the effective capacity of a tissue or
pathway, constraint `C` rises and throughput becomes nonlinear. In medicine,
this maps to saturation, resistance, plateau response, and diminishing returns.

Testable form: increasing input should stop improving output once the relevant
constraint marker rises.

## Constraint Memory Law

Biological systems can retain a history of prior load in `M_s`: fibrosis,
epigenetic locking, chronic inflammation, vascular remodeling, or scarring. A
short stress can therefore leave a long-lived constraint state.

Testable form: after equal current load, patients or models with higher prior
stress burden should show slower recovery or higher residual constraint.

## Boundary-Covariance Test

Internal flow and boundary integrity should covary in healthy systems. If `Phi`
rises while `S` falls, a tissue may enter overload even if the input looks
normal by a single biomarker.

Testable form: combined flow markers plus barrier or endothelial markers should
predict deterioration better than either alone.

## Parasitic-Drain Test

A localized node that consumes flow while contributing little systemic
responsiveness can drain surrounding capacity. Tumours, viral replication
centres, biofilms, and chronic inflammatory foci are candidate systems.

Testable form: local high-flow, low-response lesions should predict systemic
constraint burden or treatment resistance.

## Cycling-Retention Test

Recovery depends on whether repeated stress-recovery cycles lower maladaptive
memory or lock it in. Cyclic loading can be beneficial or harmful depending on
amplitude, recovery time, and baseline reserve.

Testable form: two interventions with equal total load but different temporal
structure should produce different `M_s` and `Psi_s` trajectories.

## Pharmacological Orthogonality Test

Combination therapy is expected to be safer and more effective when drugs act on
different axes, such as source flow, constraint, relaxation, or boundary
responsiveness, rather than redundantly forcing the same axis.

Testable form: cross-axis combinations should improve operating ratio without
creating a new limiting constraint; same-axis combinations should plateau or
increase adverse-event risk.

## Medicine Upgrade Candidate Metrics

These terms are proposed measurement targets, not validated biomarkers:

- `CRI`: reserve before a tissue, organ, or system enters overload.
- `CRHT`: recovery half-time after a standardized perturbation.
- `MAOS`: separation between intervention axes in a combination strategy.
- `BES`: mismatch in self, pathogen, tumor, or commensal boundary classification.
- `MLI`: persistence of maladaptive memory after the original stress is removed.
- `COCT`: transfer of constraint burden from one organ system to another.

Testable form: each metric must state a context of use, measurable proxy,
expected direction of change, failure condition, and validation dataset before
it can move beyond hypothesis status.
